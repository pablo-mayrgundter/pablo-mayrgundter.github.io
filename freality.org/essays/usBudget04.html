U.S. Budget '04: Bigger and Funner


Source: U.S. Office of Management and Budget

The United States' Government Budget for 2004 (i.e. the amount of money to be spent) is approximately $2,229,000,000,000, or $2.2 Trillion. This money comes mostly from taxing citizens and corporations, and goes mostly towards running the government and military, and paying into federal entitlement programs. $2.2T equates to about 20% of the yearly Gross Domestic Product of the U.S.. The U.S. GDP is also approximately 20% of the estimated yearly GDP of the entire World. You can compare the U.S. GDP with other countries' here.

The U.S. Budget comes from two main sources: Receipts from Taxes and "dipping into" Social Security. Receitps for 2004 are estimated at $1,922B. That leaves us $307B short, a.k.a "the deficit", which is made up for mainly by the U.S. Treasury selling the debt as long-term bonds with interest.

Where does it all go? The $2,229,000,000,000 allocated into two main categories: Mandatory and Discretionary spending.

Mandatory spending is used mainly to fund long-term programs, set up in advance and by law, such as Social Security and Medicare. Mandatory payments for 2004 will total $1,234B.

Discretionary payments for 2004 total $819B. These funds are allotted as needed every year by 15 Congressional Appropriations Committees, and are used to pay for the day-to-day operation of the U.S. Federal Government. More specifically, the U.S. Military accounts for about half, or $390B, of discretionary spending (not including President Bush's recent request for $87B more for the war in Iraq). Labor, Health and Education, together, get $135B, and the rest of the federal programs, including Agriculture, Commerce, Diplomacy, Energy, Environment, Housing, Justice, Science, Space and Transportation, receive less than $300B combined.
