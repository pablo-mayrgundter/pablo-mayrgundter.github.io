"We Support Our Troops!"


But, What are we supporting?

"We Support Our Troops" is the answer to the unspoken, but all-important question. "Do you support our troops?"

Not that one expects a true answer, but imagine I say "No." Then am I a terrorist? Or perhaps a Red Commie?

Or imagine I say "Yes." What are our troops doing that I'm supporting? Are they engaged in some sort of morally confusing situation, that could be right or wrong?

I think the truth is that such a statement is the kind of guard you need in a free society, so that people don't exercise their right and capacity for free speech. As soon as this question is heard in debate, free thinking disappears. You're either with Us or against Us. Do you support our troops? If not, imagine them pointing their guns at you.

If we were to truly ask this question, as we should, we wouldn't sleep well at night. Do we support our troops? Well, that begs the question: What really are they doing? Are they defending us from attack? If so, then yes, it is clear that we should support them, and join them if the time comes. But, what if it is the other way around? What if they're attacking others, in offense? Do we support our troops murdering people? As one of the millions who underwrite their actions, I can definitely answer that: No.

I think the truth is that our troops were murdering Iraqis recently, and we assuaged our collective conscience by asking a rhetorical question instead of a pertinent one. I call it murder because it was certainly killing, and I didn't see any Iraqis heading over here with guns blazing. Since it was not defense, it was murder.

Of course, we weren't supposed to be unprepared when it came to the secret contemplation on what our troops were doing. For most people, the rampant rallying around the flag did the trick. They toed the official party-line: the world is a complex and dangerous place, that our country has "interests" and that they're protected by our government. Our government is the highest authority. The buck stops there, and when a decision is made, it's right. Once our troops are in the field, moral questions are moot. To contemplate such questions - no matter the result - is to actually be un-American. And being American is the most important thing. As our government is the highest authority, our citizenship is our most important essence.

For the more conscientious, the President's administration proposed that what otherwise appeared to be naked aggression was actually pre-emptive defense, and deployed a cloud of thin arguments in support. It is now apparent that the rhetorical level of validity they had to achieve was that there was a remote possibility of truth. In this, the administration savagely played on the deepest fears of a post 9/11 America: any doubt was mortal doubt. Not that this excuses our response.

With 20/20 hindsight, our conscience is writhing. As the zeal of patriotism wears off, we know that our government is not the highest authority. That above authority, which can be corrupted, there are principles, which cannot. We know the responsibility for these actions ultimately rests on we, the people. Perhaps most culpable are those that voted this regime into power; in democracy, those who carry the vote carry the greatest responsibility. But the rest of us still payed for the bullets.

One-tenth of our collective tax dollar funds the largest coercive instrument on Earth, and it is being used against the loudest protests in world history. When a cry for peace is raised the world over, it should not fall flat in this land of the free and the brave. It is clear that we should settle peacefully with our neighbors, mad regime or not.

As the old saying goes: fool me once, shame on you; fool me twice, shame on me. We will all see the outcome of this war. If our leaders fail to demonstrate the validity of their reasons for war, we should not trust them to invoke such action again. Our system of government provides us with mechanisms - both swift and by due course - to change our rulers, and we can at least hope for more prudence in the next batch. But should this not happen, or should this same administration stoke the fires of war again, to send our troops into the lands of the weak, to steal resources or advantage for our pecuniary benefit, but against our common expectation for decent humanity, our protests must be more serious. These protests can start where the last left off: at our Capitol buildings where the decision can hopefully be swayed. But if it cannot, the protests should carry much farther... all the way to the naval yards and the airstrips from whence our troops depart. With the cynical motives of their commanders laid bare, the soldiers will be headed towards a certain situation, and if they do not refrain from the engagement, the troops will become warriors of aggression, forging a course of their own choosing, and we can turn the phrase on its head: "We Do Not Support Those Troops, They Are Not Ours After All!"
