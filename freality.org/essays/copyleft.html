The Choice is Clear: Copyleft

The United States Supreme Court has recently allowed virtually unlimited copyright to remain the law of the land. This is not a surprising decision, but it does finally determine the alternative. While Copyright favors decadence, Copyleft favors creativity.

As of today, when a work is Copyrighted we can predict its destiny. It will become static and held as a commercial good until after generations, it will no longer have any creative value. This was agreed to in the case, in the assesment that the marginal difference in incentive value of a work was nil in its later years. Such a work will not enrich the very public trust that helped bring it forth, and to the extent that this practice prevails, the public domain will die. Copyright will grow stronger in proportion to the vitality of each work it consumes.

As of today, we know the only work that will thrive, that will enrich the public trust, is a work that explicitly supports the intention of the United States Constitution that Copyright is granted "to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts". This goal was ignored in preference of support for the particular current mechanism of its enforcement (the the US Copyright Code), in a gesture that the Court still believes that the Legislature has the public's benefit first and foremost in their mind.

Fortunately, the public can ensure its own benefit. In a kind of legal jujitsu, the GNU Project created a way for Authors to turn Copyright against itself in support of the Public Domain. The GNU Project FAQ says:

"Copyleft is a general method..."

'To copyleft a program, we first state that it is copyrighted; then we add distribution terms, which are a legal instrument that gives everyone the rights to use, modify, and redistribute the program's code or any program derived from it but only if the distribution terms are unchanged. Thus, the code and the freedoms become legally inseparable.'

It basically creates a kind of Public Domain that cannot be parasited upon. If you take, you must give back.

By claiming Copyright, and then licensing your works under a license in accord with the goal of the Constitution interpreted by Copyleft, you will help ensure a wide and deep Public Domain for our future.

Copyleft, as a general method, must now be interpreted and practiced in a wide field, incorporating not only programming, but all creative disciplines. Copyleft is your opportunity to investment in the future plentitude of the Public Domain that the relative parasitism of Copyright threatens.

There are many people involved in this effort already. There are programmers (e.g. GNU, SourceForge), musicians (e.g. OMR) and writers who already publish their works under Copyleft licenses such as the The GNU Public License and the Open Audio License. These licenses are maintained by civil liberties groups, like the EFF, who provide their considerable experience and legal services to create and protect practical copylefting schemes. Other groups, such as the Creative Commons, further bolster this effort by providing the archival space and scholarship needed to provide a real home for the Public Domain.

Whatever your interest or capacity, the call to action is clear. Lawrence Lessig, who spoke on the behalf of the Petitioners in the case, stated in his blog today: "What the Framers of our constitution did is not enough. We must do more."
