Cato Institute's Pension Recommendations are Unsound

With the RNC convention approaching soon, and the November elections not far off, political discussion is turning up in new places. I was recently pointed at the Cato Institute by a co-worker, and though I had some expectations about what I'd find there, I thought Ok, I'm open-minded. Let's take something off their front page:

"New Study Addresses Pension Underfunding"

As soon as I saw it, I thought, Hmmm.. What do I know about the Cato institute? Conservative think tank; free markets; deregulation.

Pensions. How will they spin pension underfunding? My guess: Since pension underfunding was caused by bad investments in the tech run-up caused by misreporting of earnings via holes in stock-option accounting, Cato will want to divert the issue from the obvious solution, accounting reform, and if possible turn the pension bailouts into a win for whatever private sector industry is involved, especially if that industry is a large financial contributor to Cato.

To check this hypothesis, I first confirmed the Cato Institute's position on the FASB option accounting reform ruling.

Google: cato fasb -> http://www.cato.org/dailys/05-14-04.html "FASB is Still Wrong about Stock Options".

No surprise there. But I was surprised that their policiy analysis basically says the FASB is nuts, when even Warren Buffet conceeds options accounting is a mess. But I digress.

So, how does Cato craft it's argument? Like a preacher does for his choir.

First, they have to acknowledge the real source of the pension problems ...

"The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation, the federal agency that insures private-sector defined-benefit pension plans, had a surplus of $9.7 billion at the end of 2000 but a deficit of $11.2 billion at the end of 2003... The reasons for the PBGC's financial difficulties [are first that] plan sponsors ... invest their assets in equities rather than fixed-income securities of the same duration as the liabilities."

and completely ignore how pensions, typically conservative investors, might fail so badly as to need $20B of insurance payouts. Then divert to safer rhetorical ground ...

"First, it should enforce the current premium rules so that companies do not avoid paying premiums to the PBGC if their plans are underfunded. Second, it should modify those rules so that premiums are truly risk based."

This is the obligatory they should run their books better critique. Remember, it's the pensioners' fault, not the scam artists on Wall Street. And now for the kill ...

"Finally, Congress should allow pension insurance to be offered independent of the PBGC in the private sector or through a selfinsurance pool whose members would be jointly responsible for any deficits their plans created."

Woohoo! The crowd goes wild! University of Chicago economists stand up and cheer! Nobel's in Economics are handed out!

Instead of identifying the circumstances leading to the fleecing of pensions during the .com and proposing a fix (which would have the unfortunate effect of preventing further fleecings, probably by their clients), Cato advocates "privatizing" pension insurance. The obvious solution.

It's a magnificent scheme. Let's review.

1) Let the pensions keep investing in broken companies (again, probably clients), who can at any time fold and run with their cash (remember, we're not supposed to patch those accounting leaks),

2) then, anticipating the inevitable further breakdown of the pensions, pre-empt possible bailouts from the tax pool by shifting insurance expensive private sources (again, probably from clients). The relatively small private insurance premium will be enough to keep the appearance that the pensions are alive and avert major political attention, but won't be enough to sustain the system.

3) And finally, get rich. A severely broken pension system can be bought, pennies on the dollar, and the pension systems *in whole* can be "bailed out" by the preferred method, private corporate ownership.

Net result: pensions are available for the people who can afford them, the rich, and not for the people who need them, the working poor, who they were designed for in the first place.

Classic laissez-faire. Publicize risk, privatize profit.

Research for another time: find Cato's friends in Congress who make this work and write them considerate letters that appeal to their more humane qualities.

And the political season is just warming up!
