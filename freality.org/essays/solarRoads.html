Solar Roads

The United States has an amazing system of roads. The U.S. Department of Transportation's Bureau of Transportation Statistics quotes these figures:

  • 46,675 miles of Interstate highway.
  • 114,505 miles of other National Highway System roads.
  • 3,951,098 miles of other roads.

If you've ever walked on those roads on a hot, sunny day, you may have wondered if it made sense to try to harness that energy. After all, we spend quite a bit of money maintaining and upgrading the roads. What if our roads could be made solar collectors too? What would it cost? How much enery would it produce? Is it practical?

Photovoltaics are a very active area of research and new advances are being made frequently, yielding higher efficiences, lower cost production and more flexible material applications. Perhaps the technology is just over the horizon that makes it feasible to combine photovoltaics into the surface materials of roads and so reap a huge windfall of free energy.

Why Roads? Shared Costs

Of course, solar cells are already used to collect solar energy, in dedicated farms, on rooftops, or in your calculator. So why bother integrating them with roads? It's not clear that it is, but perhaps sharing construction and maintenance costs between these systems could result in a more efficient synthesis than possible in the two alone.

Construction and maintenance are relatively minor costs for small solar installations. At its simplest, a solar cell just needs to be laid on the ground and connected to a circuit. But imagine servicing the entire energy consumption of the United States with networks of solar cells. Then, space, infrastructure and maintenance become much larger issues. The idea of solar roads is to integrate solar collection into the same networks that are needed to support them. Instead of buying up and converting new land for use by a new solar stations in our countryside and building the new roads and power lines to reach it, how would a system built into existing roads compare?

Solar Cells

First, let's take a look around at the state-of-the art in solar cells. Recently, Kyocera of Japan has announced a milestone in large-scale solar implementation technology. Their 200-Watt module operates at an average of ~15% efficiency, and (extrapolating from Kyocera's lower-end cell prices which is basically a line of the form price=$5*watts) will cost around $1,000, or $5/Watt. Prices across the industry agree, but volume pricing goes even lower to around $3/Watt. So let's assume something like the Kyocera 200-Watt module will soon go for $3/Watt ($600 per panel) in volume.

To think about using this as a road material, let's look at the surface area this would take up. A single module is 56x39x1.5" and so a surface area of 15.16 Sqft.. Converting to the more useful size of an acre, there's 43,560 Sqft./Acre, so an acre would require about 2,872 of these modules, would cost 2872*$600 or $1,723,200 and generate 574,400 watts.

Next, how much time would this system be exposed to daylight? The most generous estimate is the hours of daylight for a given latitude. But actual peak insolation is significantly less, often totalling 5-6 hours. This calculator produces kWh predictions based on historical sunlight data for a selected region. Based off of its generation report for Austin, TX, USA, the following table lists average peak daylight hours:

Multiplied by 365 days in the year, that yearly average of daylight for this example is 20,910.85 hours. One acre of the 200-Watt modules described above would then generate 20,910.85 * 2,872 = 60055961.20 Watt hours, or 60MWh per year, for an initial outlay of $1.7M.

If the solar modules described above lasted for only 1 year, the amortized cost of their generated electricity would be $1.7M/60mWh or 2¢/Wh, or $2/kWh. Amortized over 10 years, that drops to 20¢/kWh, which is in line with the solar price indices for industry.

Refitting The Roads

First, consider that roads are constantly being repaired. The U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics, besides being the most ideal conceivable source for this analysis, estimates monthly maintenance costs for all roads in the country in the neighborhood of $55B as of October, 2002. The pavement itself is the major component of that, at 70% of cost, or about $38B/mo..

Another doc on that site gives an example road management worksheet from 1985, with an estimated cost for the total reconstruction of each square foot of road at about $16.

With those two statistics in mind, let's make a very rough estimate of the present national rate of road replacement.

To be conservative, let's say the costs have doubled since the 1985 worksheet, to $32/Sqft. for complete road replacement.

Next, at the above rate we're spending of $38B/mo., the amount of road replaced per month in the United States is approximately $38B/($32/Sqft.) = 1,187,500,000 Sqft., or assuming roads are 20 ft. wide, 1.18B/20ft/(5280 ft/mile) = 11,245 miles length of road.

That's the length of road, 0.2% of the total miles of road in the country, that is reconstructed each month in the United States. If that reconstruction could be transformed into a refitting, we would gain significant solar collection power very rapidly. Let's look at that next.

Budget and Plan

We can now guess a cost for solar roads. As a lower bound, assuming no cost to integrate solar cells into road pavement and that the solar road would last as long as regular road, solar pavement would cost $32/Sqft. for the pavement and $57/Sqft. for the solar cells or $89/Sqft. together, 2.78 times as much regular pavement. There is no firm basis for an upper bound, so let us attempt a conservative guess of an order of magnitude higher: $890/Sqft.

We've seen that existing road maintenance is large and costly, and that So, let's say the $38B monthly budget for road maintenance could be doubled to about $80B/mo.. How much solar road could be laid each month? Of the 11,245 miles of normal road that would be laid anyways, you could make 11,245/2.78=4044 miles of it solar. That's about the width of the U.S., each month. In acres, that's 4044*5280*20/(43560 Sqft./Acre)=9805 Acres.

Given the above solar generation analysis of 1 acre yielding 5MWh/month, and a monthly refitting of 9805 Acres, that's 9805 Acres * 5MWh/Acre=49025 MWh of power generation added each month, or 588,300 MWh added each year. The DOE says that the U.S. generated 3848 Million MWh in 2003.

So how long does it take to replace the generating capacity of the current U.S. system with solar roads? 6,540 years. Now that may sound like a long time, but we should hope to be around that long, and the Sun will be shining much longer :)

Also there are obvious tweaks to be considered that would substantially alter this conclusion:

  • Reinvest saved money from replaced legacy power.
  • Parameterize solar cell cost and efficiency.
  • Parameterize budget allocation.
